# HST-OUTREACH-01 — Objection Handling

---

## Objection 1: "Are you claiming to be partnered with us?"

**Heard when:** The recipient is confused about affiliation or concerned about unauthorised use of their name.

**Answer:**
"No. This is an independent concept prototype. I am not affiliated with your organisation or any specific provider. I built this to start a conversation about how the international patient journey could be improved, and I am seeking feedback and pilot discussion — nothing more."

**Why this works:** Direct, honest, no ambiguity. Repeats the key message three ways (not affiliated, independent, seeking feedback).

---

## Objection 2: "Is this medical advice?"

**Heard when:** The platform uses AI matching or suggestion language, and a provider is concerned about liability.

**Answer:**
"No. The platform does not provide medical advice, diagnosis, treatment recommendations, or outcome predictions. AI is used only for programme orientation — suggesting potentially suitable programmes based on preferences. Every suggestion is reviewed by a human coordinator and must be validated by a clinic before any proposal is shared."

**Why this works:** Clearly separates AI assistance from medical decision-making. References the human-in-the-loop safeguard.

---

## Objection 3: "Does the AI make treatment decisions?"

**Heard when:** Providers are concerned about AI replacing clinical judgment.

**Answer:**
"Absolutely not. AI is only used for programme matching — helping a patient understand which programmes might be relevant based on their stated preferences. All clinical decisions remain exclusively with the treating medical team. The platform has no role in diagnosis, treatment planning, or outcome prediction."

**Why this works:** Firm, clear boundary. Positions AI as a matching aid, not a clinical tool.

---

## Objection 4: "Do you store patient medical data?"

**Heard when:** Provider compliance or data protection teams are involved.

**Answer:**
"No patient data is stored. The prototype request form is entirely client-side — it does not send data to any server. In a future pilot, data handling would be defined in partnership with your legal and compliance teams, following Swiss FADP and GDPR requirements."

**Why this works:** Honest about current state, transparent about future needs, references the correct regulatory frameworks.

---

## Objection 5: "Is this already compliant for medical data?"

**Heard when:** The provider assumes the platform handles sensitive data.

**Answer:**
"The current prototype does not handle or store any patient data, so medical data compliance is not applicable at this stage. If a pilot were to proceed, data handling requirements would be defined together with your legal and compliance teams to ensure full compliance with Swiss FADP and applicable regulations."

**Why this works:** Does not claim compliance it does not have. Puts the responsibility where it belongs — in a partnered discussion.

---

## Objection 6: "Why would our clinic need this?"

**Heard when:** The provider feels their current website and international patient department are sufficient.

**Answer:**
"Your clinic likely already has a website and an international patient team. This concept is not a replacement — it is a structured digital layer that sits around your existing patient journey. It helps patients self-educate, compare programmes, and submit better-qualified requests before reaching your team. The goal is to reduce back-and-forth and give your team more structured enquiries."

**Why this works:** Complements rather than replaces. Acknowledges existing capability. Focuses on the specific pain point of enquiry quality.

---

## Objection 7: "How is this different from a marketplace?"

**Heard when:** The recipient assumes it is a booking platform like Booking.com for hospitals.

**Answer:**
"It is not a marketplace. A marketplace lists providers and lets users book directly. HealthStay does not allow direct booking — every request requires human coordinator review followed by clinic validation before any proposal is confirmed. It is a guided journey platform, not a booking engine."

**Why this works:** Draws a clear, accurate distinction. The "human review + clinic validation" framing eliminates marketplace confusion.

---

## Objection 8: "How is this different from your website?"

**Heard when:** The provider has their own digital presence and does not see the gap.

**Answer:**
"Your website describes what you offer. HealthStay guides a patient through the journey of discovering, comparing, requesting, and preparing for a stay — with coordinator support at each step. It is a process platform, not a content website. Think of it as the difference between a brochure and a guided tour."

**Why this works:** Simple analogy that explains the distinction without diminishing the provider's existing work.

---

## Objection 9: "Who pays for this?"

**Heard when:** The recipient assumes there is a cost to them or to patients.

**Answer:**
"For this discussion phase, I am covering the costs of development and hosting. A pilot structure would be discussed transparently — there are several possible models (co-investment, pilot grant, outcome-based), but I believe the first step is to confirm whether the concept has value for your team before discussing commercial terms."

**Why this works:** Defers pricing discussion to the right time. Signals seriousness without applying pressure.

---

## Objection 10: "What exactly do you want from us?"

**Heard when:** The recipient is unclear about your ask.

**Answer:**
"I am looking for two things: first, your feedback on whether this concept addresses real challenges your international patients face. Second, an exploration of whether a small six-month pilot would be relevant — where we would define scope, timelines, and expectations together. There is no commitment required at this stage."

**Why this works:** Clear, limited ask. Two well-defined objectives. No pressure.

---

## Objection 11: "What would a six-month pilot involve?"

**Heard when:** The provider is interested but needs concrete details.

**Answer:**
"A six-month pilot would be defined together, but a typical structure could include:
- Month 1–2: Your team reviews the prototype and provides feedback on programme content, patient journey, and terminology
- Month 3–4: We refine the journey and request workflow based on your input
- Month 5: Controlled pilot with a small number of patient enquiries routed through the platform
- Month 6: Evaluation and decision on next steps

Throughout the pilot, you remain in full control. No data is stored without agreement. AI is always optional and never involved in clinical decisions. You can pause or stop at any time."

**Why this works:** Concrete without over-promising. Emphasises provider control and optionality.

---

## Objection 12: "What if we are not ready to share internal data?"

**Heard when:** The provider is protective of their programme data and patient information.

**Answer:**
"That is completely understandable — and the pilot does not require sharing internal data. The prototype already contains public-information-level demo content. A pilot can start with that demo content and only incorporate your programme data when and if your team is comfortable. Patient data is never required at any stage."

**Why this works:** Removes the data-sharing barrier entirely. Shows flexibility and respect for their boundaries.

---

## General Principles

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Listen first | Let them finish their concern before answering |
| Validate | "That is a fair question" / "I understand why you would ask that" |
| Be honest | If you do not know the answer, say so and offer to follow up |
| Stay calm | Objections are not rejections — they are requests for more information |
| Use safe language | "Independent concept", "prototype", "pilot discussion", "for discussion purposes" |
| Do not argue | If they are not interested, thank them for their time and leave the door open |
